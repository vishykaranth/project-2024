# ⭐ 50 Principal-Level Kafka Whiteboard Answers

---

### 1️⃣ Hot partition

> *“This is a hot-partition problem. Kafka parallelism is bounded by partition count, so one overloaded partition means one overloaded consumer thread. Scaling consumers won’t help — the fix is repartitioning or redesigning the partition key.”*

---

### 2️⃣ Consumer lag spike

> *“Lag increasing on a single partition indicates either skewed traffic or slow processing. I’d check per-partition lag and consumer CPU before touching Kafka itself. Most lag issues are application bottlenecks, not broker failures.”*

---

### 3️⃣ Adding consumers didn’t help

> *“Consumers don’t increase throughput beyond the number of partitions. Extra consumers remain idle. This is a data-distribution problem, not a scaling problem.”*

---

### 4️⃣ Offset committed too early

> *“Offsets were committed before processing completed, so crashes caused messages to be skipped. Kafka did its job correctly — the consumer logic was wrong. The fix is manual commits after success plus idempotent processing.”*

---

### 5️⃣ Exactly-once misunderstanding

> *“Kafka exactly-once guarantees stop at Kafka boundaries. Once you write to a database or external system, idempotency is still required. Claiming end-to-end exactly-once without that is misleading.”*

---

### 6️⃣ Poison message

> *“A poison message blocks the entire partition because Kafka enforces ordering. The correct pattern is bounded retries followed by a dead-letter topic. Never let one message stall a partition indefinitely.”*

---

### 7️⃣ Consumer rebalance storm

> *“Frequent rebalances usually indicate aggressive autoscaling or short session timeouts. Rebalances pause consumption and amplify lag. Cooperative rebalancing and stable consumer groups reduce blast radius.”*

---

### 8️⃣ Kafka disk filled

> *“When broker disks fill up, producers block and latency spikes across the system. This cascades quickly upstream. Retention misconfiguration is the most common root cause.”*

---

### 9️⃣ Retention misconfiguration

> *“Retention is a capacity contract, not just cleanup. Misconfigured retention can either delete needed data or fill disks. We always size retention against peak traffic, not averages.”*

---

### 🔟 Schema evolution failure

> *“A required field was removed, breaking backward compatibility. Schema registry exists specifically to prevent this. Compatibility checks must be enforced in CI, not during production incidents.”*

---

### 1️⃣1️⃣ Throughput suddenly dropped

> *“Kafka throughput drops usually correlate with producer backpressure or consumer saturation. Brokers are rarely the bottleneck. I’d inspect acks, batching, and downstream processing first.”*

---

### 1️⃣2️⃣ Producer latency spike

> *“Producer latency spikes often mean brokers are throttling or partitions are overloaded. Increasing partitions or improving batching is more effective than scaling producers blindly.”*

---

### 1️⃣3️⃣ acks=all impact

> *“acks=all improves durability but increases producer latency under replication pressure. It’s a deliberate trade-off. You must provision ISR health accordingly.”*

---

### 1️⃣4️⃣ ISR shrinkage

> *“ISR shrinkage indicates broker instability or disk/network issues. It directly impacts producer latency and availability. We alert on ISR size, not just broker uptime.”*

---

### 1️⃣5️⃣ Leader imbalance

> *“Uneven leader distribution causes asymmetric load across brokers. Kafka works best when leadership is evenly spread. Leader rebalancing is a maintenance task, not an emergency fix.”*

---

### 1️⃣6️⃣ Reprocessing data

> *“Kafka allows reprocessing by design — offsets are just pointers. The challenge isn’t replaying data, it’s making consumers idempotent so replays are safe.”*

---

### 1️⃣7️⃣ Backpressure missing

> *“Without backpressure, Kafka consumers amplify downstream failures. Retries increase load and make outages worse. Backpressure is a reliability feature, not an optimization.”*

---

### 1️⃣8️⃣ DB downstream bottleneck

> *“Kafka absorbed traffic spikes, but the database couldn’t. Kafka hid the problem until lag exploded. Buffering without downstream capacity planning only delays failures.”*

---

### 1️⃣9️⃣ Partition count decision

> *“Partition count is a throughput and parallelism decision that’s hard to change later. We size partitions for peak future load, not current traffic.”*

---

### 2️⃣0️⃣ Ordering guarantees

> *“Kafka guarantees ordering per partition, not per topic. If you need global ordering, Kafka is the wrong tool. Most systems only need key-level ordering.”*

---

### 2️⃣1️⃣ Compaction misuse

> *“Log compaction is for state, not history. Using compaction for event streams causes data loss surprises. Retention and compaction solve different problems.”*

---

### 2️⃣2️⃣ MirrorMaker lag

> *“Cross-region replication is asynchronous by design. Lag is expected and must be designed for. Consumers should never assume strong consistency across regions.”*

---

### 2️⃣3️⃣ Consumer stuck but healthy

> *“A healthy consumer with growing lag usually means slow processing or blocked IO. Liveness doesn’t imply throughput. I always check thread dumps before scaling.”*

---

### 2️⃣4️⃣ Thundering herd

> *“Releasing many consumers simultaneously can overload brokers and downstream systems. Rolling deploys and rate-limited startups prevent herd effects.”*

---

### 2️⃣5️⃣ Kafka vs queue

> *“Kafka is a distributed log, not a traditional queue. Consumers own offsets, not the broker. That design enables replay but shifts responsibility to applications.”*

---

### 2️⃣6️⃣ Message duplication

> *“Kafka favors at-least-once delivery. Duplication is expected under failures. Systems must be designed to tolerate duplicates.”*

---

### 2️⃣7️⃣ Low throughput despite idle brokers

> *“Idle brokers with low throughput usually indicate producer batching or consumer processing issues. Kafka almost never becomes slow without a reason.”*

---

### 2️⃣8️⃣ Consumer CPU high, broker CPU low

> *“That’s an application problem, not a Kafka problem. Deserialization, transformation, or downstream calls are the usual suspects.”*

---

### 2️⃣9️⃣ Large messages

> *“Large messages reduce throughput and increase GC pressure. Kafka performs best with small, well-batched records. Large payloads belong in object storage.”*

---

### 3️⃣0️⃣ Retry storms

> *“Unbounded retries amplify failures. Kafka absorbs retries easily, but downstream systems don’t. Retries must be limited and observable.”*

---

### 3️⃣1️⃣ Message ordering vs scalability

> *“Ordering and scalability are trade-offs. Stronger ordering reduces parallelism. Partition design is about choosing the right balance.”*

---

### 3️⃣2️⃣ Consumer group scaling

> *“Consumer groups scale horizontally only up to partition count. Beyond that, scaling is ineffective. Partition count is the real scaling lever.”*

---

### 3️⃣3️⃣ Lag alert tuning

> *“Lag alone isn’t enough — rate of change matters. Stable lag can be acceptable, growing lag is not. Alerts should be derivative-based.”*

---

### 3️⃣4️⃣ Broker restart impact

> *“Broker restarts trigger leader elections and producer retries. With proper ISR health, this is survivable. Without it, latency spikes are guaranteed.”*

---

### 3️⃣5️⃣ Kafka upgrade risk

> *“Kafka upgrades are operationally risky due to protocol and disk format changes. We test upgrades under real traffic replay, not synthetic loads.”*

---

### 3️⃣6️⃣ Multi-tenant Kafka

> *“Multi-tenancy requires quotas and isolation. Without quotas, one tenant can starve the entire cluster. Fairness must be enforced explicitly.”*

---

### 3️⃣7️⃣ Quotas ignored

> *“Kafka quotas are safety rails, not performance tuning. If quotas trigger often, capacity planning is already failing.”*

---

### 3️⃣8️⃣ Consumer commit strategy

> *“Commit frequency trades off durability and throughput. We commit after logical units of work, not after every message.”*

---

### 3️⃣9️⃣ Stateful consumers

> *“Stateful consumers require careful rebalance handling. Losing local state during rebalances is a common source of subtle bugs.”*

---

### 4️⃣0️⃣ Kafka as buffer

> *“Kafka smooths spikes, but it doesn’t remove capacity limits. Buffers buy time, not infinite throughput.”*

---

### 4️⃣1️⃣ End-to-end latency

> *“Kafka optimizes throughput, not latency. If you need millisecond guarantees, Kafka must be carefully tuned or avoided.”*

---

### 4️⃣2️⃣ Partition reassignment risk

> *“Partition reassignment moves data and consumes network and disk IO. It should be treated like a migration, not a routine operation.”*

---

### 4️⃣3️⃣ Consumer stuck on one message

> *“One bad message can stall an entire partition. Defensive consumer design is mandatory in production systems.”*

---

### 4️⃣4️⃣ Monitoring mistake

> *“Monitoring brokers without monitoring consumers is incomplete. Most Kafka incidents originate in consumers.”*

---

### 4️⃣5️⃣ Topic explosion

> *“Too many topics and partitions strain metadata and controller performance. Kafka scales well, but not infinitely.”*

---

### 4️⃣6️⃣ Producer retries

> *“Retries without idempotence cause duplicates. Retries with idempotence increase latency. This trade-off must be explicit.”*

---

### 4️⃣7️⃣ Kafka vs stream processor

> *“Kafka stores data; stream processors execute logic. Mixing responsibilities leads to fragile designs.”*

---

### 4️⃣8️⃣ Backlog recovery

> *“Clearing backlog safely is about consumer throughput, not broker tuning. Throwing hardware at Kafka rarely fixes backlog.”*

---

### 4️⃣9️⃣ Incident ownership

> *“Kafka rarely fails silently — symptoms are visible early. Missed alerts are usually process failures, not technology failures.”*

---

### 5️⃣0️⃣ Final principal takeaway

> *“Kafka reliability depends more on consumer and partition design than broker configuration. Most outages are application-induced.”*

### 5️⃣1️⃣ Why Kafka clusters look healthy but apps fail

> *“Kafka brokers can be perfectly healthy while consumers are overloaded or blocked downstream. Broker health is necessary but not sufficient — application metrics matter more.”*

---

### 5️⃣2️⃣ Consumer stuck but no errors

> *“Kafka consumers often fail silently when blocked on IO or locks. No errors doesn’t mean no problem. Thread dumps are more valuable than logs here.”*

---

### 5️⃣3️⃣ Slow consumer vs slow broker

> *“If broker CPU and disk are low but lag increases, the bottleneck is almost always the consumer. Kafka is rarely slow without showing it.”*

---

### 5️⃣4️⃣ Producer batching impact

> *“Small batches increase latency and reduce throughput. Kafka performs best when producers batch aggressively and send fewer, larger requests.”*

---

### 5️⃣5️⃣ Compression trade-off

> *“Compression reduces network and disk IO but increases CPU usage. It’s beneficial when brokers are IO-bound, harmful when CPU-bound.”*

---

### 5️⃣6️⃣ Consumer fetch size misconfiguration

> *“Low fetch sizes increase round trips and throttle throughput. Consumers must be tuned for expected message sizes and rates.”*

---

### 5️⃣7️⃣ Fetch vs poll confusion

> *“Poll frequency doesn’t control fetch frequency. Fetching is broker-driven; polling is application-driven. Mixing them up leads to incorrect tuning.”*

---

### 5️⃣8️⃣ max.poll.interval.ms outage

> *“If processing exceeds max.poll.interval, Kafka assumes the consumer is dead and triggers rebalances. Long processing requires tuning or async handling.”*

---

### 5️⃣9️⃣ Heartbeat failures

> *“Missed heartbeats usually indicate CPU starvation or blocking calls, not network issues. Heartbeats are cheap unless the app is unhealthy.”*

---

### 6️⃣0️⃣ Deserialization bottleneck

> *“Deserialization is CPU-heavy and often underestimated. A slow deserializer can cap consumer throughput even when Kafka is idle.”*

---

### 6️⃣1️⃣ Large consumer groups

> *“Large consumer groups increase rebalance cost and coordination overhead. Beyond a point, adding consumers reduces stability.”*

---

### 6️⃣2️⃣ Coordinator overload

> *“Group coordinators can become hotspots in large clusters. This manifests as rebalance delays and commit timeouts.”*

---

### 6️⃣3️⃣ Offset commit latency

> *“Slow offset commits usually indicate coordinator pressure or network issues. Excessive commit frequency amplifies the problem.”*

---

### 6️⃣4️⃣ Commit on every message

> *“Committing offsets per message is almost always a mistake. It reduces throughput and provides little additional safety.”*

---

### 6️⃣5️⃣ Read-committed isolation

> *“Read-committed avoids aborted transactional messages but increases latency. It’s a correctness trade-off, not a free optimization.”*

---

### 6️⃣6️⃣ Transaction timeout misuse

> *“Long transaction timeouts increase resource usage and delay cleanup. Kafka transactions should be short and scoped tightly.”*

---

### 6️⃣7️⃣ Kafka Streams failure

> *“Kafka Streams hides complexity but doesn’t remove it. State stores, changelogs, and rebalances introduce new failure modes.”*

---

### 6️⃣8️⃣ State store recovery

> *“State recovery time depends on changelog size, not traffic. Large state stores increase restart time significantly.”*

---

### 6️⃣9️⃣ Exactly-once Streams myth

> *“Kafka Streams exactly-once only applies within the topology. External side effects still require idempotency.”*

---

### 7️⃣0️⃣ Consumer vs Streams

> *“Plain consumers offer more control and fewer surprises. Kafka Streams trades transparency for convenience.”*

---

### 7️⃣1️⃣ Broker GC pauses

> *“Broker GC pauses manifest as leader unavailability and request timeouts. This usually points to mis-sized heaps or excessive metadata.”*

---

### 7️⃣2️⃣ Controller instability

> *“Controller churn indicates ZooKeeper or quorum instability. This affects partition leadership cluster-wide.”*

---

### 7️⃣3️⃣ ZooKeeper impact

> *“ZooKeeper failures don’t stop Kafka immediately, but they prevent recovery actions. That’s when small issues escalate.”*

---

### 7️⃣4️⃣ Metadata refresh storms

> *“Frequent metadata updates increase network chatter and CPU. Topic churn and auto-creation amplify this.”*

---

### 7️⃣5️⃣ Topic auto-creation risk

> *“Auto-creating topics hides mistakes and creates operational debt. Explicit topic management is safer at scale.”*

---

### 7️⃣6️⃣ Under-replicated partitions

> *“Under-replicated partitions reduce durability and increase producer latency. This is a warning sign, not just a metric.”*

---

### 7️⃣7️⃣ Replication factor choice

> *“Replication factor is a durability SLA decision. Higher isn’t always better — it increases write amplification.”*

---

### 7️⃣8️⃣ Cross-AZ traffic cost

> *“Replication across AZs increases network cost and latency. Kafka durability is not free.”*

---

### 7️⃣9️⃣ Broker rack awareness

> *“Rack awareness prevents correlated failures but increases placement complexity. It must be tested, not assumed.”*

---

### 8️⃣0️⃣ Segment size tuning

> *“Small segments increase index overhead; large segments slow recovery. Segment size affects both performance and operability.”*

---

### 8️⃣1️⃣ Log retention delete lag

> *“Retention cleanup is asynchronous. Disk pressure can build up before cleanup catches up.”*

---

### 8️⃣2️⃣ Compaction lag

> *“Compaction lag means old keys remain longer than expected. This affects consumers relying on latest state.”*

---

### 8️⃣3️⃣ Tombstone misuse

> *“Tombstones require retention and compaction alignment. Otherwise deletes appear to ‘not work’.”*

---

### 8️⃣4️⃣ Partition reassignment pain

> *“Reassignments compete with normal traffic. Doing them during peak load increases risk.”*

---

### 8️⃣5️⃣ Consumer pause/resume

> *“Pause/resume is a powerful backpressure tool. It’s safer than shutting down consumers during overload.”*

---

### 8️⃣6️⃣ Graceful shutdown importance

> *“Without graceful shutdown, consumers cause unnecessary rebalances and duplicate processing.”*

---

### 8️⃣7️⃣ Rolling deploys

> *“Rolling deploys must respect consumer group stability. Killing too many consumers at once creates lag spikes.”*

---

### 8️⃣8️⃣ Schema registry outage

> *“Schema registry outages break producers and consumers at runtime. It’s a critical dependency, not optional infra.”*

---

### 8️⃣9️⃣ Caching schemas

> *“Clients should cache schemas aggressively. Runtime schema lookups increase latency and failure surface.”*

---

### 9️⃣0️⃣ Security overhead

> *“TLS and auth increase CPU and latency. Security must be capacity-planned, not bolted on later.”*

---

### 9️⃣1️⃣ ACL explosion

> *“Fine-grained ACLs improve security but increase management complexity. Tooling matters more than policy count.”*

---

### 9️⃣2️⃣ Kafka quotas surprise

> *“Quotas throttle suddenly and look like random latency spikes. If quotas trigger, you’re already at capacity.”*

---

### 9️⃣3️⃣ Multi-cluster Kafka

> *“Multiple smaller clusters reduce blast radius but increase operational overhead. This is a resilience trade-off.”*

---

### 9️⃣4️⃣ Kafka as source of truth

> *“Kafka is durable but not authoritative by default. Retention policies decide how ‘truthful’ it really is.”*

---

### 9️⃣5️⃣ Rewind vs replay risk

> *“Rewinding offsets is easy; replaying safely is hard. Idempotency is the real challenge.”*

---

### 9️⃣6️⃣ Backlog after outage

> *“Clearing backlog requires consumer throughput exceeding producer throughput. Scaling must account for this delta.”*

---

### 9️⃣7️⃣ Metrics that matter

> *“Per-partition lag, consumer CPU, and processing time matter more than broker uptime.”*

---

### 9️⃣8️⃣ Alert fatigue

> *“Kafka generates many noisy metrics. Alerts should reflect user impact, not internal counters.”*

---

### 9️⃣9️⃣ Kafka incident pattern

> *“Most Kafka incidents start small, grow quietly, and explode late. Early lag detection prevents outages.”*

---

### 1️⃣0️⃣0️⃣ Principal takeaway

> *“Kafka reliability is an application responsibility. The platform is resilient — misuse is what causes outages.”*
