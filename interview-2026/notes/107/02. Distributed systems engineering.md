Here’s a **curated “Distributed Systems Engineering – Top 100 one-liners”** — the kind of lines that show *real scars*, not textbook knowledge.

---

## 🧠 Fundamentals

1. Distributed systems fail in ways single-node systems never imagine.
2. Network calls are slow, unreliable, and not free.
3. If it can fail, it will fail—at the worst possible time.
4. Latency matters more than throughput for users.
5. Time is an illusion in distributed systems.
6. Partial failure is the norm, not the exception.
7. Coordination is harder than computation.
8. You don’t have “the system,” you have *many versions of it*.
9. Retries turn small failures into big ones.
10. “Eventually” is a contract, not a promise.

## ⏱️ Time, Ordering & Consistency

11. There is no global clock.
12. Clock skew breaks assumptions silently.
13. Ordering is expensive—buy it only when needed.
14. Causality matters more than timestamps.
15. Exactly-once is usually “at least once + idempotency.”
16. Linearizability is a luxury.
17. Eventual consistency is a design choice, not a bug.
18. Read-your-writes is often all users actually need.
19. If you didn’t design for staleness, you will debug it.
20. Consensus trades availability for correctness.

22. CAP is about *trade-offs*, not absolutes.
23. You always choose between consistency and availability—partition or not.
24. Availability without correctness is just fast wrong answers.
25. Consistency without availability is a system no one can use.
26. Partition tolerance isn’t optional.
27. Fail open vs fail closed is a business decision.
28. A timeout is not a failure; it’s an uncertainty.
29. Healing after partitions is harder than surviving them.
30. Split-brain is a symptom of unclear authority.
