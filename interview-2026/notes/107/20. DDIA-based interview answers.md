# 🎤 DDIA-Based Interview Answers (Principal+)

---

## 1️⃣ *How do you think about scalability?*

**Answer**

> “Scalability is about how a system behaves as load grows, not how big it is today.”

> “I focus on throughput, tail latency, and skew.”

> “Most systems fail due to uneven load, not total volume.”

> “I look for the first coordination point — that’s usually the scaling limit.”

---

## 2️⃣ *What’s harder: scaling reads or writes?*

**Answer**

> “Writes are harder because they require coordination.”

> “Reads can be cached and replicated; writes must establish order.”

> “Replication makes writes slower but reads faster.”

> “Eventually, write amplification and coordination dominate.”

---

## 3️⃣ *When would you choose eventual consistency?*

**Answer**

> “When availability and latency matter more than immediate correctness.”

> “Most user-facing systems tolerate staleness better than outages.”

> “Eventual consistency shifts complexity to the application layer.”

> “The key is making anomalies visible and acceptable.”

---

## 4️⃣ *How do you handle hot keys?*

**Answer**

> “Hot keys are a symptom of coupling data semantics to partitioning.”

> “Hashing assumes uniformity — reality gives skew.”

> “You can’t out-scale a hot partition.”

> “I prefer key indirection or bounded fan-out with explicit ordering trade-offs.”

---

## 5️⃣ *What consistency model do most systems actually need?*

**Answer**

> “Stronger than eventual, weaker than linearizable.”

> “Causal consistency covers most real-world expectations.”

> “Linearizability is expensive and usually unnecessary.”

> “Consistency is a latency choice.”

---

## 6️⃣ *How do you think about caching?*

**Answer**

> “Caching trades freshness for performance.”

> “Invalidation is the hard part.”

> “Caches amplify traffic during failure.”

> “Every cache introduces new failure modes.”

---

## 7️⃣ *How do you reason about distributed transactions?*

**Answer**

> “Distributed transactions add coordination and ambiguity.”

> “Two-phase commit doesn’t fail fast — it fails stuck.”

> “Most systems are better off with idempotency and compensation.”

> “Atomicity across services is often an illusion.”

---

## 8️⃣ *What causes tail latency?*

**Answer**

> “Queues, retries, GC, network variance, and coordination.”

> “The slowest component defines the user experience.”

> “Averages hide failure.”

> “Tail latency grows non-linearly under load.”

---

## 9️⃣ *How do you design for failure?*

**Answer**

> “I assume partial failure is normal.”

> “I design for timeouts, retries, and fallbacks.”

> “Failure handling must be explicit.”

> “Silent failures are worse than crashes.”

---

## 🔟 *How do you think about replication lag?*

**Answer**

> “Replication lag is user-visible inconsistency.”

> “Failover always risks stale or lost writes.”

> “Reads from replicas trade correctness for latency.”

> “Lag must be part of correctness reasoning.”

---

## 1️⃣1️⃣ *When do you shard data?*

**Answer**

> “When a single node becomes the bottleneck.”

> “Partitioning is about reducing contention, not just size.”

> “Resharding is expensive and risky.”

> “The shard key is a long-term contract.”

---

## 1️⃣2️⃣ *How do you approach schema evolution?*

**Answer**

> “Schemas evolve independently across services.”

> “Backward compatibility is mandatory.”

> “You can’t assume synchronized deploys.”

> “Schema mistakes are permanent.”

---

## 1️⃣3️⃣ *What’s your view on exactly-once semantics?*

**Answer**

> “Exactly-once is expensive and fragile.”

> “Most systems need idempotency, not EOS.”

> “EOS shifts complexity into the control plane.”

> “Duplicates are cheaper than coordination.”

---

## 1️⃣4️⃣ *How do you evaluate new data systems?*

**Answer**

> “I start with failure modes, not features.”

> “I look at operational complexity.”

> “I ask how it behaves under skew.”

> “I want to know how hard migration is.”

---

## 1️⃣5️⃣ *What’s the biggest mistake teams make in distributed systems?*

**Answer**

> “Assuming components fail independently.”

> “Ignoring skew.”

> “Over-optimizing for the happy path.”

> “Underestimating operational complexity.”

---

## 1️⃣6️⃣ *How do you think about time in distributed systems?*

**Answer**

> “Time is unreliable.”

> “Clocks drift, messages reorder, delays vary.”

> “I avoid relying on synchronized clocks.”

> “I design using causality, not timestamps.”

---

## 1️⃣7️⃣ *Batch vs Stream processing?*

**Answer**

> “Batch is simpler and more reliable.”

> “Streams give lower latency at higher complexity.”

> “Streams are stateful systems pretending to be pipelines.”

> “Exactly-once in streams is a coordination problem.”

---

## 1️⃣8️⃣ *What signals do you monitor first?*

**Answer**

> “Tail latency.”

> “Error rate by dependency.”

> “Backlog growth rate.”

> “Skew indicators.”

---

## 1️⃣9️⃣ *How do you reason about correctness?*

**Answer**

> “Correctness is contextual.”

> “I define acceptable anomalies explicitly.”

> “I prefer predictable inconsistency to unpredictable outages.”

> “Correctness must be end-to-end.”

---

## 2️⃣0️⃣ *Final DDIA-Level Closing Answer*

> “Distributed systems don’t fail because of missing features.
> They fail because of hidden assumptions about data, time, and coordination.”
