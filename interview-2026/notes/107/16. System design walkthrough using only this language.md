# 🧠 SYSTEM DESIGN WALKTHROUGH

*(Principal+ Language Only)*

---

## 1️⃣ Frame the Problem (Before Any Design)

> “Before designing anything, I want to understand where scale pressure comes from.”

> “I’m more worried about skew than volume.”

> “Traffic won’t be uniform, and failures won’t be clean.”

> “Let’s identify irreversible decisions early.”

> “I’ll focus on bottlenecks, coordination, and change over time.”

---

## 2️⃣ Clarify Workload Shape

> “What’s the peak-to-average ratio?”

> “Are events bursty or steady?”

> “Which clients are untrusted?”

> “What’s the worst-case producer behavior?”

> “Does traffic concentrate on a few keys?”

> “Which path dominates cost?”

> “What grows faster — event count or event size?”

---

## 3️⃣ Identify the Scalability Unit

> “What is the unit of scale here?”

> “What can be parallelized independently?”

> “What cannot be parallelized?”

> “Where does ordering actually matter?”

> “If one unit is slow, does it block others?”

> “What becomes hot under skew?”

---

## 4️⃣ Call Out the First Red Flags

> “This introduces global coordination.”

> “This smells like a hot partition risk.”

> “We’re assuming traffic is evenly distributed.”

> “This design caps throughput on a single path.”

> “Leadership here becomes a scalability liability.”

> “This works until retries kick in.”

---

## 5️⃣ Partitioning Strategy (Without Saying ‘Partition’)

> “We need ownership boundaries that don’t overlap.”

> “Work must be isolated so slow paths don’t block fast ones.”

> “The key choice matters more than the infrastructure.”

> “If one key becomes hot, it shouldn’t stall unrelated work.”

> “We need a way to change ownership boundaries later.”

> “What assumptions does this key encode?”

---

## 6️⃣ Ordering & Consistency Tradeoffs

> “Do we really need ordering everywhere?”

> “Where is correctness local versus global?”

> “This level of consistency feels accidental.”

> “We’re paying coordination cost on the hot path.”

> “Can correctness converge instead of synchronize?”

> “What breaks if ordering is relaxed?”

---

## 7️⃣ Failure Modes (Explicitly)

> “How does this fail under partial outages?”

> “What happens when one dependency is slow, not down?”

> “Do failures cascade or stay contained?”

> “What happens when retries amplify load?”

> “Which failures are silent?”

> “What’s the degraded mode?”

> “How does the system behave when humans are asleep?”

---

## 8️⃣ Backpressure & Load Control

> “Where does pressure surface first?”

> “How does the system say ‘no’?”

> “What happens if producers ignore signals?”

> “Are queues bounded?”

> “What protects the system from well-intentioned retries?”

> “What happens under sustained overload?”

---

## 9️⃣ Migration & Evolution (Critical)

> “We don’t have a migration story yet.”

> “This decision is hard to undo.”

> “How do old and new paths coexist?”

> “Can we migrate incrementally?”

> “What happens mid-migration?”

> “How do we validate correctness during change?”

> “Is rollback safe and cheap?”

---

## 🔟 Observability as a Design Requirement

> “What metric tells us this is unhealthy?”

> “How do we detect hot spots early?”

> “Do we see per-ownership-unit saturation?”

> “What’s the leading indicator of failure?”

> “Can we explain incidents from metrics alone?”

> “What alerts before users complain?”

---

## 1️⃣1️⃣ Cost & Efficiency Reality Check

> “What’s the cost per event at peak?”

> “What scales cost faster than traffic?”

> “Can one bad client amplify cost?”

> “What’s the marginal cost of retries?”

> “Is this predictable under growth?”

---

## 1️⃣2️⃣ Organizational Fit

> “Who owns this in production?”

> “Who gets paged when this breaks?”

> “Is this operable by oncall at 3 AM?”

> “Does the org structure match the architecture?”

> “Is operational complexity creeping in?”

---

## 1️⃣3️⃣ Stress the Design with Reality

> “What happens when one ownership unit is 100× hotter?”

> “What happens during deploys?”

> “What happens when traffic shape changes?”

> “What happens when assumptions break?”

> “Does this degrade gracefully or catastrophically?”

---

## 1️⃣4️⃣ Final Design Evaluation

> “This scales technically, but is it operable?”

> “We’ve reduced coordination on the hot path.”

> “Failure is isolated, not amplified.”

> “The system forgives bad decisions.”

> “The cost of change is acceptable.”

> “This design ages reasonably.”

---

## 🎯 Closing (Principal+ Signature)

> “The biggest risk here isn’t whether this works today — it’s how expensive it is to change tomorrow.”

> “I’d start simple, instrument heavily, and evolve based on real skew.”

> “Scale will expose assumptions, so the design must tolerate being wrong.”

---

You:

* Treated **change** as a first-class requirement

This is **exactly** how Principal / Distinguished engineers think and speak.
