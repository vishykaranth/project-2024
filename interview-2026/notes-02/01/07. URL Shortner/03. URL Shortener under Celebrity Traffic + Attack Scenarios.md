## 🎯 3–4 Minute “Perfect Answer”

**URL Shortener under Celebrity Traffic + Attack Scenarios**

> **Opening (20–30 seconds — set the frame)**
> “When a celebrity tweets a link, the problem isn’t just scale — it’s *skew*. In distributed systems, 10 million QPS on one key is far more dangerous than 10 million QPS spread evenly. So I design for two distinct failure modes: **hot keys** and **high-cardinality random traffic**.”

---

### 🔥 Case 1: Hot Key (Celebrity Tweet) — *Performance Failure*

> **What breaks first**
> “A single cache shard melts. Even with a large Redis cluster, consistent hashing sends all traffic for one key to one node. If the key expires, you also get a cache stampede that can take down the database.”

> **How I design it**

1. **Edge-first serving** using CloudFront or Workers with `stale-while-revalidate`

   * The CDN serves stale redirects while only *one* request revalidates the origin
   * This collapses 10M QPS into ~1 QPS at the backend
2. **L1 in-process caching** in the app layer

   * Each instance caches the mapping for ~30 seconds
   * Redis load becomes proportional to *fleet size*, not traffic
3. **Optional Redis key salting**

   * Replicate hot keys across shards to avoid single-node hotspots

> **Result**
> “Hot traffic never reaches my core infrastructure. Performance is stable and cost is near zero.”

---

### 💣 Case 2: High-Cardinality Traffic — *Financial Failure*

> **What breaks first**
> “With Lambda and DynamoDB On-Demand, nothing crashes — the credit card does. At 10M QPS for 30 minutes, you can rack up a six-figure bill without any technical alarms.”

> **How I stop bankruptcy**

1. **Bloom Filter at the edge**

   * Export all valid short IDs into a replicated Bloom filter
   * If the key *definitely doesn’t exist*, return a 404 at the edge
   * This drops most random traffic without touching Lambda or DynamoDB
2. **AWS WAF rate-based rules**

   * Block IPs requesting thousands of *unique* URLs per second
   * Apply geo-blocks when traffic is clearly non-customer
3. **Provisioned DynamoDB with ceilings**

   * I cap spend and accept controlled 503s over unbounded cost
   * Adaptive capacity helps isolate abusive partitions

> **Result**
> “The system degrades gracefully instead of bankrupting the company.”

---

### 🚨 Incident-Mode “Nuclear Option” (30 seconds)

“If the attack is sophisticated, I apply **semantic filters**. For example, only allow redirects for links created in the last 48 hours — which covers viral events but cuts off 99% of random-key attacks. During an incident, correctness is secondary to survival.”

---

### 🧠 Close (10–15 seconds)

> “So my design philosophy is simple:
> **Hot keys are a performance problem solved with caching and distribution.
> Random keys are a cost-control problem solved with negative filtering and hard limits.**
> I design for both from day one.”
