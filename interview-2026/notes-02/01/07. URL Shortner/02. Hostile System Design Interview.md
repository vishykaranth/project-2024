For a world-class global URL shortener, your **p99 latency target for redirects** should be **< 50ms**.

To achieve this "instant" feel regardless of where the user is (London, Tokyo, or New York), you cannot rely on a single central database. The "100ms rule" (Paul Buchheit's Gmail principle) dictates that any interaction over 100ms feels sluggish; since a redirect is just the *start* of a page load, your service must consume as little of that budget as possible.

---

### The Latency Hierarchy

Depending on where your data lives, your p99 will fluctuate significantly:

| Tier | Strategy | Expected p99 | Why? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **L0 (Edge)** | **Cloudflare Workers / Fastly Compute** | **< 20ms** | Redirection logic runs at the CDN POP. The "database" (KV store) is replicated globally to the edge. |
| **L1 (Global Cache)** | **Regional Redis / ElastiCache** | **30ms – 50ms** | Requires a trip from the user to a regional data center. Very fast, but subject to internet backbone jitter. |
| **L2 (Cross-Region)** | **Origin Database (RDS/Postgres)** | **200ms – 500ms** | **FAIL.** A user in Australia hitting a US-East-1 DB will never hit a <100ms p99 due to the speed of light. |

---

### How to actually hit < 50ms Globally

To maintain a consistent p99 at 100k TPS, you must eliminate the "Origin" from the read path entirely:

1. **Global Edge Key-Value (KV):** Use a service like **Cloudflare KV** or **DynamoDB Global Tables**. When a URL is shortened in the US, it is replicated to every global region within seconds.
2. **DNS Georouting:** Use **Route 53 Geolocation routing** to ensure the user's request hits the nearest AWS region or Edge location.
3. **Zero-Inertia Redirects:** Do not use a heavy framework. Your redirect service should be a "naked" function (Golang or Rust) that simply reads from a local cache and returns a `301/302` header.
4. **Async Analytics:** Never write to your analytics database (clicks, user-agent, etc.) during the request. Fire an asynchronous event to **SQS or Kinesis** and return the redirect immediately.

---

### What usually ruins your p99?

If your average is 10ms but your p99 is 500ms, look for:

* **Cold Starts:** If using AWS Lambda, provisioned concurrency is required to avoid the 1-second "warm-up" for the slowest 1% of users.
* **Cache Stampedes:** When a viral link expires from the cache, 100k requests might hit the DB at once. Use "Singleflight" or "Request Coalescing" to ensure only one request goes to the DB while the others wait.

This is the "consistency vs. availability" cliff. In a global system, **deleting** a link for abuse is much harder than **creating** one because you are fighting against the very caches that make the system fast.

### 1. The Global Takedown: Propagation Time

If you use a standard global Edge KV (like Cloudflare KV or DynamoDB Global Tables), here is the reality of your "Delete" timeline:

* **Regional Propagation (The "Hot" Path):** Within the region where you issued the delete, the change is usually visible in **< 1 second**.
* **Global Propagation (The "Cold" Path):** Across the world, it typically takes **15 to 60 seconds**.
* **The Cache Problem:** Even if the database is updated, the **Edge Cache** (CDN) might have a TTL (Time-To-Live) of minutes or hours. Without active invalidation, a malicious link could remain "live" in Tokyo for an hour after you deleted it in New York.

**The Fix:** You must issue a **Global Purge** command to your CDN/Edge layer simultaneously with the database delete. This brings the "Total Takedown" time down to **~5–10 seconds** globally.

---

### 2. Blast-Radius Control: When Replication Lags

Replication lag is inevitable. If your US-East region goes down or slows down, your Japan region might still be serving "Zombie" links that should have been deleted. To control the blast radius, you use a **Two-Tier Verification** strategy:

#### Tier 1: The Fast Redirect (Edge)

The Edge function checks the local KV.

* **Link Exists:** Redirect immediately (p99 < 50ms).
* **Link Missing:** Return 404.

#### Tier 2: The "Suspicious Link" Bloom Filter (The Blast-Radius Guard)

Since you can't check a central DB for every request, you use a **Compressed Bloom Filter** replicated to the edge.

* **How it works:** This filter contains the IDs of all "Blacklisted" or "Recently Deleted" links. It is tiny (a few MBs) and replicates much faster than the full KV store.
* **The Logic:** Even if the local KV says "Link is Active," the Edge Worker checks the Bloom Filter. If the ID is in the "Abuse Filter," the Worker **forces a synchronous check** against the origin or blocks the request entirely.

| Failure Scenario | Impact Without Control | Impact With Bloom Filter |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Replication Lag** | Malicious link stays live for minutes. | Blocked instantly at the edge. |
| **Regional Outage** | Users see stale/unsafe data. | Edge rejects links not in the "Safe" list. |
| **Database Corruption** | Massive redirect failures. | Limited only to the links modified during the lag. |

---

### 3. Summary of Failure Semantics

1. **Delete Intent:** Admin marks link as `ABUSE`.
2. **Primary Write:** DynamoDB Global Table (Region A) is updated.
3. **Broadcast:** An EventBridge event triggers a **Global CDN Purge** and updates the **Global Bloom Filter**.
4. **Edge Enforcement:** Even if the local Japan KV is stale, the Bloom Filter catch blocks the redirect.

# 🔴 Hostile Question 3 — Hot Key / Viral Link Meltdown

> “A celebrity tweets a short URL.
>
> That single key now gets **10 million QPS** for 30 minutes.
>
> **What breaks first in your system, and how do you stop one key from melting everything?**”

⚠️ I’m testing:

* Hot-key amplification
* CDN vs origin protection
* Cache stampede control
* Cost-awareness (not just survival)

👉 **Answer step-by-step.**
Assume adversarial traffic + legitimate traffic mixed together.

(Go.)

When a celebrity tweets a link, you aren't just dealing with "scale"—you’re dealing with a **Hot Key** problem. In a distributed system, 10 million QPS on a *single* key is more dangerous than 10 million QPS spread across 10 million different keys.

### 1. What breaks first?

* **The Cache Node (Redis/Memcached):** Even if you have a cluster of 100 Redis nodes, all 10 million requests for that one short URL (`/xyz`) will hash to the **same single node**. That node’s CPU or Network Bandwidth will saturate instantly, causing it to "melt" while the other 99 nodes sit idle.
* **The Cache Stampede:** If that key expires even for 100ms, thousands of concurrent requests will "miss" the cache and hit your Database simultaneously (the Thundering Herd). Your DB will collapse under the weight of 10,000+ identical queries for the same URL.

---

### 2. How to stop one key from melting everything

To survive this, you must move from **Single-Node Caching** to **Multi-Layered Distribution**.

#### **Step A: Edge-Level "Stale" Serving (The 0ms Path)**

At 10M QPS, the request shouldn't even reach your AWS/Cloud infrastructure.

* **Service:** Cloudflare Workers or AWS CloudFront + Lambda@Edge.
* **Mechanism:** Set a high **S-Maxage** (CDN cache) and a low **TTL**.
* **The Secret:** Use `stale-while-revalidate`. When the cache expires, the CDN serves the *old* destination to the 10M users while it sends exactly **one** request to your origin to get the new one. This reduces 10M QPS to **1 QPS** at your origin.

#### **Step B: Local In-Memory Caching (L1)**

Inside your application servers (Go/Node/Java), use an **In-Process Cache** (like `go-cache` or `Caffeine`).

* Instead of calling Redis for every request, the server stores the mapping in its own RAM for 10–30 seconds.
* If you have 1,000 application instances, your Redis now only sees **1,000 total requests** every 30 seconds, regardless of whether you have 10M or 100M users.

#### **Step C: Hot Key "Salting" (If you MUST use Redis)**

If you cannot use local memory, you can replicate the hot key across all Redis nodes by appending a random suffix.

* **Write:** Store the URL as `xyz:1`, `xyz:2`, ... `xyz:100` across all nodes.
* **Read:** The client randomly picks `xyz:{rand(1,100)}`.
* **Result:** You spread the 10M QPS load across your entire 100-node cluster instead of killing one.

---

### 3. The "Blast Radius" Architecture

To ensure the celebrity tweet doesn't kill the service for "regular" users:

| Feature | Implementation |
| --- | --- |
| **Request Coalescing** | Use a "Singleflight" group. If 5,000 threads on one server need the same URL, only one thread fetches it; the other 4,999 wait for that single result. |
| **Dedicated "Hot" Shards** | Move high-traffic keys to a separate "Burst" cluster so that even if it dies, the "Standard" cluster remains responsive. |
| **Adaptive Throttling** | If a single key exceeds 1M QPS, trigger an "Emergency Static Redirect" at the WAF/CDN level that doesn't even touch your code. |

# 🔴 Final Hostile Question — Cost & Abuse (Last Filter)

This is the *bar-raiser* question.

> “An attacker realizes your system handles hot keys well.
>
> They generate **10 million distinct short URLs** and hit each one **once per second**.
>
> CDN won’t help.
>
> **How do you prevent this from bankrupting you?**”

⚠️ I’m testing:

* Cost-aware rate limiting
* Abuse prevention
* Write-path vs read-path economics
* Whether you can say **no** to traffic

👉 **Answer like you’re defending the AWS bill.**

This is the **"High Cardinality"** or **"Random Key"** DDoS attack. It is the architectural equivalent of a "Death by a Thousand Cuts."

Since the attacker is hitting 10 million *unique* keys, your CDN and L1 caches are useless (0% hit rate). Every single request will "miss" the cache and penetrate deep into your infrastructure, hitting your Lambda and your Database.

If you are using **AWS Lambda + DynamoDB On-Demand**, you won't "crash"—the system will perfectly scale to handle the attack. **The failure is financial:** At 10M QPS for 30 minutes, you are processing 18 billion requests. At standard AWS rates, this could trigger a bill in the **$100,000+ range** before your morning coffee is cold.

---

### 1. What breaks first?

* **The "Credit Card":** You won't hit a technical limit; you'll hit a budget limit.
* **Downstream Bottlenecks:** While DynamoDB scales, any non-serverless components (like an older SQL DB for analytics or a logging sidecar) will fall over instantly.
* **CloudWatch Logs:** Ingesting 10M logs per second is surprisingly expensive and can actually trigger its own throttling.

---

### 2. How to prevent the bankruptcy

You cannot rely on "scaling" to fix a cardinality attack. You must use **Negative Filters** and **Structural Defense.**

#### **Step A: The Bloom Filter "Shield" (The $0 Defense)**

Before you ever hit DynamoDB or a Lambda, you use a **replicated Bloom Filter** at the Edge.

* **The Logic:** A Bloom Filter is a memory-efficient structure that can tell you if a key *might* exist or *definitely does not* exist.
* **The Implementation:** You periodically export the list of "Valid Short IDs" into a Bloom Filter (e.g., 20MB for 100M links).
* **The Result:** When the attacker hits `/random_string_123`, the Edge Worker checks the filter. If the filter says "Definitely doesn't exist," the Worker returns a `404` at the Edge.
* **Cost Savings:** An Edge 404 is ~100x cheaper than a Lambda execution + DynamoDB Read.

#### **Step B: AWS WAF "Request Squashing"**

You apply a **Rate-Based Rule** at the WAF level, but with a twist:

* **IP-Level Throttling:** If a single IP is requesting 1,000 *different* URLs per second, it’s not a celebrity fan—it’s a bot. WAF can automatically block that IP for 1 hour.
* **Regional Geofencing:** If the 10M QPS is coming from a region where you don't even have customers, flip the "Off" switch for that country at the WAF level.

#### **Step C: Use "Standard" DynamoDB (Not On-Demand) with a Ceiling**

If you know your "Peak Real Business" is 100k TPS, you can switch from "On-Demand" to **Provisioned Capacity** with a hard upper limit.

* **The Trade-off:** Instead of a $100k bill, your legitimate users will see `503 Service Unavailable` errors alongside the attacker.
* **The Strategy:** Use **Adaptive Capacity**. DynamoDB will try to isolate the "bad" traffic to specific partitions, keeping the "good" keys alive even while the system throttles the attacker.

---

### 3. The "Emergency Switch" (The Nuclear Option)

If the attack is sophisticated (distributed across millions of IPs), you use **URL Pattern Signatures**:

1. **Analyze:** Real celebrity links usually have a specific "age" or "pattern" (e.g., they were created in the last 24 hours).
2. **Filter:** Add a temporary rule to the Edge: "Only process requests for keys created in the last 48 hours. Reject everything else."
3. **Result:** You've just cut 99% of the attacker's "Random Key" surface area while keeping your most valuable traffic (the viral tweet) alive.

| **Hot Key (Celebrity)** | CDN / L1 Cache | Near $0 |
| **Random Key (Attacker)** | Bloom Filter / WAF | Low (Edge Costs) |
| **Financial/Billing** | Provisioned Limits | Capped |
